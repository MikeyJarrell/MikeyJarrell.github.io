Understanding the Sliders

Tier Configuration

Choose how many tiers to use for classifying plants (2–6). More tiers provide finer distinctions; fewer tiers simplify decisions. The color gradient runs from blue (best) to orange (worst) and is designed to be colorblind-friendly. Default: 4 tiers (Certified, Conditional, Remediation, Excluded) with thresholds at 20, 50, and 80.

Assessment vs. Measurement

This slider controls how much weight to give visual assessments (checklist observations) vs. pollution measurements (air, soil, blood lead data). At 0%, only assessment data matters. At 100%, only measurements matter. Increase measurement weight if you trust environmental data more than visual inspections. Default: 33% measurement (assessment 67% | measurement 33%).

Pathway Weights

Lead emissions come from four pathways: stack (furnace exhaust), slag (waste disposal), acid (battery electrolyte), and fugitive (dust escaping the building). Weights reflect each pathway’s share of maximum potential emissions. Stack dominates because uncontrolled furnaces emit the most lead. Adjust these if you believe certain pathways matter more or less than our defaults suggest. Default: Stack 60 | Slag 18 | Acid 10 | Fugitive 12.

Measurement Weights

We estimate emissions from three data sources. Air captures current operations — what’s happening right now. Soil captures historical emissions — cumulative over years. Blood directly measures human exposure — the outcome we ultimately care about. If you trust blood measurements as ground truth, increase its weight. If you think historical practices matter, increase soil. Default: Air 50 | Soil 25 | Blood 25.

Population Discounting (Delta)

Delta controls how much we weight nearby vs. distant population. At delta = 1.0, all population counts equally regardless of distance — only total nearby population matters. At delta = 0.90, distant population is heavily discounted — plants in sparse areas get lower scores. This is a value judgment: do emissions matter if no one is nearby to absorb them? Default: 0.99.

Rate vs. Total

This is perhaps the most important conceptual choice. Rate (harm per battery) answers: “If we had a battery to recycle, should we send it here?” Total (current damage) answers: “How much harm is this plant causing right now?” We default to rate because shutting down a high-throughput, low-rate plant could push batteries to worse alternatives. But if your goal is to identify the biggest current problems, weight toward total. Default: 0 (pure rate).
